  TASK EXPLANATION — GEOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS OF RESTAURANT LOCATIONS
  Data Analytics Internship Project
==============================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS THE TASK?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The task is to perform a geographic analysis on a dataset of restaurants. The
two core objectives are:

  1. Plot the locations of restaurants on a map using their longitude and
     latitude coordinates so that the global spread of establishments is
     clearly visible.

  2. Identify any spatial patterns or clusters of restaurants — meaning groups
     of restaurants that are concentrated in particular cities, regions, or
     geographic zones — and explain what those patterns reveal about the market.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHY IS THIS TASK IMPORTANT?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Geographic analysis of business locations provides critical intelligence for:

  • Market sizing — Understanding where the most restaurants are concentrated
    tells analysts where competition is highest and where the market is mature.

  • Expansion planning — Identifying regions with relatively few restaurants
    helps decision-makers spot underserved markets.

  • Consumer behaviour — Restaurant density reflects where people live, work,
    and spend, making it a proxy for urban activity patterns.

  • Quality benchmarking — Overlaying ratings onto the map reveals whether
    high-concentration markets also deliver high quality, or whether density
    comes at the cost of customer satisfaction.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HOW WAS THE DATASET PREPARED?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The raw dataset contained 9,551 restaurant records with 21 attributes each.
Before any analysis, three cleaning steps were applied:

  1. SENTINEL REMOVAL — Records where both longitude and latitude were exactly
     (0, 0) were dropped. These are commonly used as placeholder values when
     actual coordinates are unknown and would incorrectly anchor restaurants
     at the Gulf of Guinea in the Atlantic Ocean.

  2. BOUNDS VALIDATION — Any coordinates outside the valid geographic range
     (latitude ±90°, longitude ±180°) were removed as data-entry errors.

  3. NULL DROPS — Records missing city name, latitude, or longitude were
     excluded since they cannot contribute to spatial analysis.

After cleaning, 9,052 valid records remained across 141 cities in 15 countries.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT ANALYTICAL METHODS WERE USED?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

METHOD 1 — COORDINATE SCATTER MAPPING
  Every restaurant was plotted as a point on a world coordinate grid (longitude
  on the x-axis, latitude on the y-axis). Each point was colour-coded by its
  aggregate customer rating using the "plasma" colour scale (dark purple = low
  rating, bright yellow = high rating). This immediately reveals where
  restaurants are geographically concentrated.

METHOD 2 — DBSCAN SPATIAL CLUSTERING
  DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) is an
  algorithm that groups nearby data points into clusters without requiring the
  number of clusters to be specified in advance. It works by:

  • Converting decimal-degree coordinates to radians (required for the
    haversine distance metric, which measures distances on a sphere rather
    than a flat plane).

  • Scanning the dataset with a 50 km search radius (epsilon). Any restaurant
    within 50 km of at least 30 other restaurants is considered part of a
    dense region.

  • Labelling restaurants that are not within any dense region as "noise"
    (isolated or standalone locations).

  The algorithm found 4 distinct geographic clusters and 1,423 noise points.

METHOD 3 — CITY-LEVEL DEEP DIVE
  The five cities with the most restaurants (New Delhi, Gurgaon, Noida,
  Faridabad, Ghaziabad) were examined individually with scatter plots.
  Restaurants within each city were plotted and coloured by rating, revealing
  spatial rating gradients within each urban area.

METHOD 4 — CONVEX HULL BOUNDARY MAPPING
  A convex hull is the smallest polygon that encloses all points in a set.
  Drawing convex hulls around the top-6 clusters clearly shows the geographic
  footprint (physical area) covered by each cluster, making it easy to
  compare their spatial extents.

METHOD 5 — STATISTICAL DASHBOARDS
  Bar charts, scatter plots, and histograms were produced to summarise:
  • Cluster sizes (restaurant count per cluster)
  • Cluster quality (average rating per cluster)
  • Price range versus customer rating relationship
  • Top 10 most common cuisine types globally

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT WERE THE FINDINGS?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

FINDING 1 — HEAVY GEOGRAPHIC CONCENTRATION IN SOUTH ASIA
  New Delhi alone accounts for approximately 57% of all records. The entire
  National Capital Region (NCR), which includes New Delhi, Gurgaon, Noida,
  Faridabad, and Ghaziabad, holds over 70% of the dataset. This reflects
  either the market's genuine size or a data collection bias toward South Asian
  sources. Either way, the NCR is the dominant geographic market in this dataset.

FINDING 2 — FOUR DISTINCT SPATIAL CLUSTERS
  DBSCAN identified four clusters at a 50 km search radius:
  • Cluster 0: NCR Core (New Delhi) — largest and densest
  • Cluster 1: NCR Suburbs (Gurgaon / Noida) — adjacent to Cluster 0
  • Cluster 2: Southeast Asia (Manila) — second-largest international cluster
  • Cluster 3: Europe / International — smaller cluster with high average ratings

  The remaining 1,423 isolated restaurants are spread across cities in the
  United Kingdom, United States, Canada, New Zealand, Australia, South Africa,
  Brazil, Turkey, Sri Lanka, and the UAE.

FINDING 3 — PREMIUM ESTABLISHMENTS RATE HIGHER
  Analysing average customer rating by price tier reveals a clear positive
  correlation. Restaurants in Price Range 4 (luxury) receive consistently
  higher ratings than those in Price Range 1 (budget). This suggests that
  customers either have higher satisfaction at premium venues, or that they are
  more generous with ratings when spending more.

FINDING 4 — CUISINE DOMINANCE REFLECTS MARKET ORIGIN
  North Indian cuisine is the most frequently occurring cuisine type, followed
  by Chinese and Fast Food. This distribution directly reflects the geographic
  concentration of the dataset in North Indian urban centres. International
  markets (Europe, Southeast Asia, Oceania) show greater cuisine diversity,
  with local specialities representing a larger share of listings.

FINDING 5 — RESTAURANTS CLUSTER AROUND COMMERCIAL ZONES
  Within individual cities, restaurant coordinates cluster tightly around
  commercial corridors and central business districts. Residential periphery
  areas show visibly lower density. This confirms that restaurant operators
  prioritise high-footfall, commercially active urban zones.
